About this simulation

Flocking boid simulation models the collective behavior of birds, fish, or other animals. Each "boid" follows three simple rules:

Separation:

Boids avoid collisions by steering away from nearby boids.

Alignement:

Boids match the average velocity of nearby boids.

Cohesion:

Boids move towards the center of mass of nearby boids.

These rules were developed by Craig Reynolds in 1986 and result in emergent behaviors like flocking or schooling, where individual agents interact locally to create cohesive group motion. More


The quadtree algorithm

efficiently divides a 2D space into smaller regions, reducing the number of comparisons needed for collision detection by organizing objects into hierarchical structures. It's particularly useful in scenarios with many objects, such as flocking simulations or games, where traditional collision detection methods would be computationally expensive. More
